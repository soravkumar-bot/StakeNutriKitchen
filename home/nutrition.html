Nutrition Fundamentals

Understanding what you eat empowers better choices. This page covers macronutrients, hydration, and portion awareness — in plain language, without the jargon.

ℹ️ This content is informational only. It is not personalized medical or nutritional advice. Please consult a registered dietitian for guidance specific to your health needs.
The big three

Understanding macronutrients

Macronutrients are the three main categories of nutrients that provide energy and building blocks for the body.

🥩

Protein

Proteins are made of amino acids and are essential for tissue repair, immune function, enzyme production, and maintaining lean muscle mass. Found in both animal and plant sources.

Calories per gram4 kcal/g

Good sources: Chicken, fish, eggs, lentils, chickpeas, tofu, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese.

General guidance: Needs vary significantly by individual. A common population guideline is 0.8g/kg body weight per day as a minimum, with higher amounts for active individuals.

🍞

Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates are the body's preferred energy source, particularly for brain function and physical activity. Quality matters — complex carbs from whole foods provide sustained energy and fiber.

Calories per gram4 kcal/g

Complex carb sources: Oats, sweet potato, brown rice, quinoa, whole grain bread, legumes.

Simple sugars: Found in fruit (with fiber) and refined foods. The fiber in whole fruit slows sugar absorption — minimally processed sources are generally preferable.

🥑

Fats

Dietary fat is essential for hormone production, absorption of fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K), brain health, and cell membrane integrity. Not all fats are the same.

Calories per gram9 kcal/g

Unsaturated fats: Olive oil, avocado, nuts, seeds, oily fish — associated with heart health.

Saturated & trans fats: Found in processed foods, butter, and fatty meats. General guidance is to limit trans fats and moderate saturated fat intake.

Often overlooked

Hydration basics

Water is involved in nearly every body process. Staying adequately hydrated supports digestion, energy levels, and cognitive function.

💧

General daily target

About 1.5–2.5 litres of fluid per day for most adults is a common guideline — though needs vary significantly with activity, climate, and body size. Thirst is a reasonable indicator.

🏃

During exercise

Drink water before, during, and after physical activity. For sessions over 60–90 minutes of intense exercise, electrolyte replacement may be beneficial.

☕

Counting towards fluids

Tea, coffee, milk, and juice all contribute to fluid intake. Caffeinated drinks have a mild diuretic effect but still contribute net fluid.

🔍

A simple hydration check

Pale yellow urine generally indicates good hydration. Dark yellow suggests you may need more fluids. Clear urine may indicate over-hydration.

Practical guidance

Understanding portion sizes

Portion awareness — not obsessive measuring — can help maintain a balanced diet. These are rough guides using familiar references.

✋

Palm of hand

Roughly one portion of protein (e.g., chicken, fish, tofu) — about 85–100g for many adults.

👊

Closed fist

Approximately one serving of starchy carbohydrates — cooked rice, pasta, or oats (~150g cooked).

🤏

Thumb

Roughly a serving of fat-dense foods like cheese, nut butter, or butter (~20–30g).

🖐️

Two cupped hands

A generous serving of salad, leafy greens, or non-starchy vegetables — eat freely and often.

💡 A note on portion guidance

These visual guides are rough approximations. Actual nutritional needs depend on your body size, age, activity level, and health goals. They're intended to support awareness — not to encourage obsessive food measurement. If you have concerns about your relationship with food or portion control, speaking with a healthcare professional is always the right step.
